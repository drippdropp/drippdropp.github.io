Applying Fermat's Little Theorem

Suppose we want to compute 2438(mod7)24^{38} \pmod{7}2438(mod7). Anything other than 111 to the 383838th power is very large and could be quite challenging to calculate, especially by hand.

By Fermat's Little Theorem, if ppp is prime and aaa and ppp are co-prime, then,

ap−1≡1(modp) .a^{p-1}\equiv 1 \pmod{p}\,. ap−1≡1(modp).

First of all, we're working in modulo 777 and since 24>724 > 724>7, we know that 2438≡33824^{38} \equiv 3^{38}2438≡338 since 24≡3(mod7)24 \equiv 3 \pmod{7}24≡3(mod7).

Applying Fermat's Little Theorem, we know that 36≡1(mod7)3^{6} \equiv 1 \pmod{7}36≡1(mod7). By the law of exponents and division algorithm, we can re-write 3383^{38}338 as 36⋅6+23^{6\cdot 6 + 2}36⋅6+2. Since we know that 363^{6}36 is congruent to 111 in modulo 777, then (36)6⋅32≡(1)6⋅32=32(3^{6})^6 \cdot 3^2 \equiv (1)^6\cdot 3^2 = 3^2(36)6⋅32≡(1)6⋅32=32. Therefore, we can re-write our original equation and easily solve.

2438≡32≡2(mod7) 24^{38} \equiv 3^2 \equiv 2 \pmod{7} 2438≡32≡2(mod7)

We can easily verify this using the Julia function powermod which takes three arguments, xxx, yyy, and mmm such that,

xy(modm) . x^y \pmod{m} \,.xy(modm). 
powermod(24, 38, 7)
> 2
