Problems with Julia

Why I am confident that Julia is production-ready

Two of the most common reasons that I tend to hear regarding why people don't want to use Julia in work-related projects or in their production environments are,

  1. Julia is still a young language,

  2. Julia doesn't have widespread adoption.

Even though I'm a bit of a Julia fanboy, it's not without numerous reasons. Furthermore, among those aforementioned reasons are, in my opinion, reasons which specifically invalidate the two "problems" I often hear.

Julia is still a young language

This is usually a compound reason since there are just a lot of things that happen over time. Bugs get worked out, communities grow, other people run into the problems we may run into and post their solutions on the web, and things just happen to improve as a whole. It's just the natural course for any language.

That being said, it's worth noting that while I am not completely against taking risks, I tend to be fairly extensive on the mitigation side, bordering obsessive over what could go wrong and trying to think of ways to combat those potentials in the case they do happen to occur, even if when they seem unlikely.

So why does Julia's youthfulness not bother me at all? It's because there aren't many languages which have given me as few problems as Julia with respect to version conflicts or integrating external packages and libraries.

Prior to Julia hitting version 1, issues were a bit more frequent due to larger syntactical changes from release to release. As of v1.6.1, there are a few libraries I'll run across, generally for really specific, niche purposes, which are written in v0.6 or similar and haven't been updated, where I'll run into compiling issues.

I've also run into some problems compiling Julia from source on OSX, albeit I've never had any compiling issues on any Linux flavours.

Not to mention, the few issues that I have occasionally run into with Julia, have been easily fixed where I didn't even need to do any web searching, but was able to check an error log or message and make a small adjustment which got me back on track. Furthermore, I've never run into any problems which created security vulnerabilities or problems which impacted the functionality or output of my code. I compare this to numerous other languages which are currently deeply engrained into the current software development landscape which have done serious damage or created vast security vulnerabilities, and sometimes silently.

It may be a young language, but the people in the Julia community tend to be extremely talented scientists and engineers.

With Julia still being fairly young and without wide adoption, often times the problems I have run into don't have easily resolvable solutions and have required a decent bit of web searching or even needing to post on Github or discourse and await a response from a fellow Julia-friendly.

That all being said, I rarely run into problems. Even NodeJS being older than Julia and with its massive community and forced, non-negotiable adoption by all, I am always running into conflicts, silently created vulnerabilities, or other serious issues. With Julia, I run into a miniscule fraction of issues comparably and they are easily resolved.
