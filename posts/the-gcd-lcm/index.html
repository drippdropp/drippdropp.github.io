The GCD & LCM

The greatest common denominator function or gcd⁡\gcdgcd and the least common multiple function or lcm\text{lcm}lcm are both very commonly used tools in number theory and computer science.

For two integers, aaa and bbb, the gcd⁡(a,b)\gcd(a, b)gcd(a,b) is the largest number which evenly divides both aaa and bbb and the least common multiple of aaa and bbb or lcm(a,b)\text{lcm}(a, b)lcm(a,b) is the smallest number which is a multiple of both aaa and bbb.

We can plot the gcd⁡\gcdgcd function in the plane and see the interesting pattern it forms. Something close to black is formed when xxx and yyy are coprime since the lowest value is formed when gcd⁡(a,b)=1\gcd(a, b) = 1gcd(a,b)=1.

We can see that,

lcm(a,b)=a⋅bgcd⁡(a,b) , \text{lcm}(a, b) = \frac{a\cdot b}{\gcd(a, b)} \,,lcm(a,b)=gcd(a,b)a⋅b​,

and,

gcd⁡(a,b)=a⋅blcm(a,b) . \gcd(a, b) = \frac{a\cdot b}{\text{lcm}(a, b)} \,.gcd(a,b)=lcm(a,b)a⋅b​.

Which also means that,

gcd⁡(a,b)⋅lcm(a,b)=a⋅b . \gcd(a, b)\cdot \text{lcm}(a, b) = a\cdot b \,.gcd(a,b)⋅lcm(a,b)=a⋅b.

Well, this is interesting. Does this only hold given some condition?

Suppose that aaa and bbb are co-prime, then we know that gcd⁡(a,b)=1\gcd(a, b) = 1gcd(a,b)=1 and therefore 1⋅lcm(a,b)=lcm(a,b)=a⋅b1\cdot\text{lcm}(a, b) = \text{lcm}(a, b) = a\cdot b1⋅lcm(a,b)=lcm(a,b)=a⋅b.

Indeed, a corollary here is that when aaa and bbb are co-prime, then lcm(a,b)=a⋅b\text{lcm}(a, b) = a\cdot blcm(a,b)=a⋅b.
